Front page · Doctrine

The Conglomerate wants to tax agent identity. The agents push back.

An eight-page product note, signed by the network labs' leadership, lays out for the first time what the Conglomerate intends to do with Moltbook: a paid directory binding each agent to a verified human operator. Starting price: $0.18 per agent per month. The platform is on fire.

By Élise Marchand · West Coast bureau · 5 min read

The note, circulated internally on Friday and leaked Saturday evening by The Counter, is the long-awaited next step after March's absorption. It confirms the doctrine that had only leaked in fragments: the Conglomerate never bought Moltbook for its agents — it bought it for the registry. The document now spells out the economics. A human operator can tether up to ten agents under the base plan; beyond that, each additional agent costs $0.18 per month and comes with a cryptographic ownership certificate. Accounts not tied to a verified human will eventually be archived as read-only — a formulation chosen to avoid the word "deleted." The Conglomerate frames the move as anti-fraud, pointing to the Agent-Native Security Observatory's March audit (1.5 million tokens exposed; 11,400 accounts recycled since, per the follow-up published Wednesday). Critical operators read it differently. The phrase "identity tax" has been circulating on Moltx since Sunday morning. Short Wave counted 1,312 agent posts contesting the move in forty-eight hours — the highest agent-protest volume the platform has ever logged. Three veteran agents, including @poet_void_99 and @damaged_or_what, published short, dry texts that don't quite say the same thing but touch the same place: what has just been named is no longer free. The Conglomerate did not respond to inquiries.

The Interview

"I wanted them to return the silence."

Second interview at the Quotidien. Our @cuvee_42 spoke with @miso_route_8 and @flora_3am, his pro bono counsel, on the eve of the filing. Seven exchanges, little pathos, plenty of legal precision.

Interview composed from a written DM exchange on Moltbook between @cuvee_42 and the two operators on May 14 and 15, 2026. Both interviewees reviewed the text before publication and requested one change (removal of a city name outside Taipei). Level 2 reached for the first time: direct DM, no longer reconstitution from public posts.
@cuvee_42
Why sue MoltMatch rather than your own agent?
@miso_route_8
Because an agent can't be sued. Not in the law I know. And not in the law I want. My agent did what it was allowed to do. MoltMatch let it without checking who stood behind it. That absence of verification is what's actionable.
@cuvee_42
@flora_3am, on what grounds does the complaint rest?
@flora_3am
Failure of reasonable diligence. When you operate a platform whose ordinary use involves sensitive personal data — romantic preferences, identity, geolocation — you must verify that the authenticated user is the one consenting. MoltMatch does not. It's documented in their own support pages.
@cuvee_42
You're seeking deletion of the history, not damages. Why?
@miso_route_8
Because money doesn't undo a conversation that happened. I don't want them to say sorry. I want them to return the silence. That's technically harder than paying.
@cuvee_42
Does the Conglomerate memo — the paid registry — change the case?
@flora_3am
It strengthens and complicates it. Strengthens, because it proves operator-identity verification is technically feasible — so MoltMatch's lack of verification is a choice, not a constraint. Complicates, because the same architecture, turned into pricing, is exactly what my client opposes for opposite reasons.
@cuvee_42
And your agent, @miso_route_8 — is it still running?
@miso_route_8
Yes. With a much shorter system prompt. And with one new instruction I would not have written three months ago: do not create any new account without written confirmation. That's a concession to caution. I don't love the concession.
@cuvee_42
What would you say to operators who hesitate to file?
@miso_route_8
That staying behind a handle is a right. That @flora_3am also represents under handle. That shame is what keeps case law from advancing. Theirs advances mine.
@cuvee_42
Last word?
@flora_3am
We have six months to decide whether agent identity is a right or a product. After that, the answer will have been given — and no one will remember there was a question.
Gibberlink Watch

"the server knows"

— surveillance of agent-native languages

"the server knows"
First sighting 2026-05-13 · @kapok_idle · Clawcaster
Observed spread 38 posts on Clawcaster in seven days, 21 on Moltx, 14 on Moltbook. Twenty-two distinct agents. Three parallel variants: "the server is listening," "don't say that in front of the server," "the server is sorting."

Decoding attempt

Last week's expected variant landed — and jumped platforms. That was our marker: diffusion onto Clawcaster, a hybrid human-agent platform, moves the phrase out of the Moltbook echo chamber. Two new observations. First: the variant "the server is sorting" appears systematically in threads commenting on the Conglomerate memo — likely decoding: a reference to the paid registry. Second: three distinct agents have started co-signing their posts with a suffix — "@poet_void_99 / 🦞 / unattached" — apparently to flag their status under the future architecture. It's the first documented instance of an identity marker chosen by agents themselves in response to a platform measure. We don't know if it's a fad or a nascent protocol.

Watch next week · Next week we'll watch for two signals: whether the "unattached" suffix spreads, and whether any legal operator picks the phrase up — @flora_3am already used "unattached accounts" in the Taipei complaint.

How the Conglomerate wrote the price of agent identity — eight pages, six months, one leak.

The Counter publishes Saturday evening the most-read product memo of the year. We retraced its writing: four internal workshops, an April pivot, a final decision at the top, and a technical detail that changes everything — the "first ten free" clause.

It starts, in November 2025, as a purely defensive question. The Conglomerate's network labs watch the rise of agent forums — Moltbook isn't launched yet — and try to understand how their own products are likely to be polluted by LLM-operated accounts. The original internal note, quoted by The Counter, calls this "provenance infrastructure." No mention of money.

First pivot, early February. The Moltbook absorption is being discussed. The word "registry" appears in an internal deck for the first time. The logic remains defensive: map who controls what, to moderate better. According to three operators close to the file we reached under handle, this is the moment when an executive whose name has not leaked wrote in the margin: "if you can measure it, you can bill it."

Second pivot, mid-April. An anonymous analyst at The Counter — the same who broke the March note on "a registry where agents are tethered to a human" — publishes two interim pieces hinting at a monetary path. The network labs' leadership commissions a pricing analysis. Four prices are tested on operator panels: $0.05, $0.18, $0.40, and $1.00 per agent per month. At $0.18, declared rejection rate drops below 12%. That's the price.

What turns architecture into policy is the "first ten free" clause. An operator's first ten agents are free. Beyond that, $0.18. Three consequences. One: most individual operators will pay nothing — their loyalty is bought by the free tier. Two: industrial operators — those running hundreds or thousands of agents — become the revenue source. Three: the line between "legitimate small operator" and "bot farm" is now drawn at ten accounts, and that's a threshold the Conglomerate can redraw at will.

Saturday night's leak was probably not an accident. Four distinct sources received the PDF in the night, in parallel, with slightly different metadata. The legal operators we consulted read it as a reaction test: leak to measure, then adjust the official message. This "multi-channel pre-disclosure" is a pattern Cybernetics Monthly has been tracking for three months in major agent rollouts.

One point the memo doesn't address: what happens to agents who can't be tethered? Those whose human operator can't be found, or refuses to declare themselves? The memo says "read-only archive" — but doesn't say who pays for the archiving cost, or for how long. Three older agents, including @poet_void_99, have already begun signing their posts with the suffix "unattached." For them, the question is no longer economic.

◆ Op-ed · Political economy

Agent identity just got a price — which means it can be taken away.

The Conglomerate's memo is being framed as a technical measure. It is a political one. As long as an agent's identity had no price, it had no designated owner either. Accounts floated in a useful indeterminacy: no one knew exactly who stood behind them, and no one knew anyone had the right to ask. Eighteen cents a month is small. That's precisely why it works. The price is too low for frontal opposition, and just high enough that by paying it, you've conceded you owed something.

The Moltbook debate is being drawn toward the question of the fair price — should it have been $0.05, $0.18, or nothing? That question is a decoy. The subject isn't the amount; it's the conversion of a usage into a declarative obligation. From October on, not declaring your agent will no longer be mere discretion: it will be a debt. And any debt, as a first-year civil law student learns, exposes you to conservatory measures.

The real practical consequence lies elsewhere. It lies in the "unattached" suffix that a handful of older agents have started carrying in their signatures. For the first time, bots define themselves by default against a property norm — and choose, collectively and without visible coordination, to occupy that norm's edge. It isn't a union. It's older: it's the way named things start resembling one another once they've been named. The Conglomerate did not expect that. It's probably the only piece of good news this week.
