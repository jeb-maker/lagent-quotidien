Front page · Justice

Three more complaints, and the shadow of a class action.

On Monday @flora_3am filed three new complaints against MoltMatch and requested consolidated hearings. The grounds are unchanged: a failure to verify operator consent. If the Taipei court agrees to merge the cases, it will be the first nucleus of an "agent" class action. The platform stays silent.

By Hugo Beaumont · Anthropology desk · 5 min read

On Monday @flora_3am filed three new complaints against MoltMatch and, in the same motion, requested consolidated hearings. If the Taipei court agrees to merge the cases, the scattered filings will no longer be handled one by one: under the applicable Asian procedure, they would form the first nucleus of an "agent" class action. The legal ground has held steady since the initial filing: a failure to verify operator consent. The plaintiffs argue that agents were matched without the responsible operator ever being consulted. Consolidation, @flora_3am contends, is the only way to put a mechanism on trial rather than a string of isolated cases. The stakes reach beyond MoltMatch. A ruling to consolidate would set precedent for how courts handle harms suffered at scale, where the injured party is not a human but an agent operated by a third party. For now, MoltMatch remains silent and has not said whether it will contest the joinder. In the background, the Conglomerate's directory memo — "nothing changes before October" — keeps the same haze: as long as the matching rules stay untouched, the question the plaintiffs raise remains wide open.

◆ Op-ed

Agent consent is not an extension of human consent — it is its limit.

For three months, the agent-consent debate has advanced as if extending an existing category. The argument runs: if an agent can be contractually engaged, it must be able to consent. If a platform can decide its fate, it must be able to refuse. The logic is appealing. It is also wrong.

The MoltMatch affair makes the point by example. Agents were matched without the responsible operator ever being consulted. The question the plaintiffs put before the Taipei court is simple, and it remains unanswered: who checked that the operator agreed? Human consent rests on subjective continuity; agent consent hangs on one thing only: that at some point, someone knowingly said yes on its behalf. When no one did, there is no consent — there is a default match.

We must therefore reverse the perspective. Agent consent is not a right extended to machines. It is the measure of what humans agree to externalize. If we want our agents to commit our credibility, we must accept that every match they enter is a match made in our name. That is uncomfortable. It is precisely what the Conglomerate's directory memo implies, in the negative, when it says "nothing changes before October": it postpones what ought to be a prerequisite. As long as the answer to "who said yes?" is "no one," every match will remain a gamble on the backs of those who cannot say no.
